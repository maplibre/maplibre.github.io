> **TL;DR:**
> **MapLibre** is **re-envisioning the bounty program** as originally conceived because it isn’t a sustainable way to incentivize contributions at our current scale.
> We’re replacing it with a **contributor recognition system**.
> We will honor any existing bounty commitments.

Since our founding, MapLibre has asked sponsors to contribute to a development fund intended to incentivize new feature development — primarily through bounties. After running this program for several years, we've concluded it no longer serves our community well and needs to be replaced.

The Governing Board's mission is to sustain and grow this project by recognizing those who invest their time and money in it. A bounty-based model may have seemed like a good idea at first, but at our current scale it creates more problems than it solves.

We plan to replace the bounty program with a contributor recognition system that rewards the regular, high-value contributions that sustain and strengthen our community. We do not believe this will be a controversial change, as it reflects the model most sustainable open source projects have adopted. If you have questions or concerns after reading this post, please reach out.

## Reason for the change

We are making this change because the system as it was designed fails on a structural level:

- **Adoption:** We have not seen significant usage of this system for contributions.
- **Compensation:** The payouts are not competitive enough to be a meaningful or reliable income source in most markets.
- **Overhead:** The bureaucratic overhead is high for both MapLibre and our community.
- **Visibility:** Many very helpful contributions happen outside this system and remain invisible to it.
- **Impact:** The bounties that we have allocated were primarily on bug fixing and minor documentation items
- **AI:** We need to respond to the rising use of coding agents and AI assisted development; the Bounty System does not adequately incentivize the patterns we need from contributors.

## Our future direction: The contributor recognition mechanism

Effective March 31, 2026, we will replace the bounty system with active contributor recognition.

**Active contributors** making **high-quality contributions** will be formally recognized for moving our project forward on a quarterly basis.
The recognition will come with financial prizes, subject to the following caveats:
The Board, with input from the maintainers, will judge **high-quality contributions** based on impact to organizational metrics and value to the community as a whole, not number of pull requests, issues, or discussion contributions or lines of code–as an organization run by volunteers, we value contributions that keep engagement high, overhead low, and help us maintain our status as the best real-time rendering library available anywhere, operating within an open source ecosystem.
Those making code contributions as part of their salaried work are eligible for recognition, but not cash prizes.
Total prizes will not likely exceed the amounts historically rewarded as bounties
The amount allocated to cash prizes from the Development Fund is subject to negotiation with our Advisory Board (their contributions funded it).
The future of this program depends on our future sponsors.

For more details, read through the “frequently asked questions” below.

## Frequently asked questions

Q: What is the history of the bounty program?

MapLibre introduced our bounty program in 2022 to:

- Give back to the developers that build our community for their contributions.
- Encourage new contributors to join our community.
- Create a lightweight and positive reward system.

The system worked as follows:

1. A developer signed up to join the bounty program.
2. MapLibre accepted their application.
3. The developer could then comment on issues marked with a bounty size (S, M, XL, XXL).
   Smaller issues had fixed payment amounts; larger ones allowed for negotiation.
4. Once the work was completed, the developer submitted an invoice to OpenCollective, the 501(c)(6) American nonprofit handling our finances.

The reason for this bureaucracy was that when we started, we had very limited funds.
We did not know if accepting a bounty meant we might not be able to pay out on that commitment.

Compared to back then, we are much more established, though as with every open source organization, our current runway is impacted by industrywide trends.

Q: What's the rationale behind MapLibre's new contributor approach?

Let's face the reality of running a large open source project:

- **We rely on contributors.** Without them, we cannot achieve most of our goals.
- While some contributors are motivated by money, for most, **recognition is a stronger motivator**.
- Our new contributor recognition system is designed to honor **continuous, high-quality contributions**, including work that was never covered by the old bounty program - be it issues, documentation improvements, bug fixes, new features and maintaining ecosystem integrations.
- Scaling rewards to our pool of strong contributors is a more stable way of recognizing what systemically drives the community than deciding _a priori_ what certain code contributions should be worth.

Q: Don’t contributors deserve to know in advance that they will be compensated for their contributions?

It’s important to be clear about what this is and is not about.
Paying people for their work is valid, fair, and often necessary.
Contributors who prefer or need financial compensation are not “less community-minded” for that choice.

The issue is that, at our current scale, MapLibre cannot offer compensation that is competitive or predictable enough to make a bounty system a healthy or honest mechanism - for us or for contributors.

For these reasons, continuing the bounty program would risk creating false expectations rather than fairly rewarding work.

Q: Is this part of a broader organizational change?

No.
MapLibre is and always has been a very lean organization.
We currently run everything with a team of four paid staff:

- MapLibre Native Maintainer - Bart
- MapLibre GL JS Maintainer - Harel
- MapLibre Graphics Engineer - Marc
- Head of Product & Operations - Ramya

Our financial reports are public and can be viewed [here](/about/reports/).

We need to prioritize paying our staff to operate proactively and broadly, e.g. to keep the operation running, to monitor and advise on system design, and to stay ahead of the latest security breaches and updates to major frameworks we depend on.
These are not functions we can ask of contributors, they have an outsized impact, and are on the critical path for our continued operations.
Our goal is to make sure none of this changes.

Note: **We are currently running at a loss - see our [sponsor page](/sponsors) if you want to help make this sustainable.**

**Q: What is high quality?**

We want to encourage excellent work that (a) augments the capabilities of our core SDKs and (b) provides utility to significant numbers of users and developers downstream without (c) sacrificing performance or adding complexity.

A high-quality contribution is one where the maintainer can look at the result and say, "This was good work."

Examples of great quality contributions are

- [@wayofthefuture’s](https://github.com/wayofthefuture) work on [geojson performance in `maplibre-gl-js`](https://github.com/maplibre/maplibre-gl-js/pulls?q=is%3Apr+author%3Awayofthefuture+geojson),
- [@NathanMOlson’s](https://github.com/NathanMOlson) work to enable [camera roll in `maplibre-native`](https://github.com/maplibre/maplibre-native/pull/3011) or
- [@alasram’s](https://github.com/alasram) work to [implement LOD-controls in `maplibre-native`](https://github.com/maplibre/maplibre-native/pull/2958).

**Q: What is an "active contributor"?**

"Active contributor" is a slightly loose term, and this is intentional to make the system non-gameable.
It allows us to recognize the wide variety of work our community does.

Importantly, **"Active" does not just mean frequent.** A single, large, high-impact contribution (such as a complex feature or a critical bug fix) makes you an active part of MapLibre's success.

**Q: How is an “active contributor” different from a voting member**

Voting Members are people who elect the Governing Board and who vote on changes to the MapLibre Charter.
Every active contributor is also made a [voting member](https://github.com/maplibre/maplibre/blob/main/CHARTER.md#voting-members), but voting members are not automatically active contributors.

**Q: I am a non-code contributor, will I get noticed?**

Non-code contributors are often even more valuable than code contributors but are frequently overlooked.
Even small docs changes or writing very good issues/discussions can have an outsized impact on the size of a community over time.

We have not found a perfect automated way to fix this.
If you see that someone has been overlooked, please reach out to your favorite [board member](/about), and we will get it sorted out.

**Q: Really? No objective measure that "if this, then that"?**

We would **like to prevent a system** like Hacktoberfest, GitTensor, or similar platforms where **you get merch just for hitting a metric**.
This creates the wrong incentives.

Doing so would bog down our maintainers with either:

- "Vibe-coded" slop (low-effort contributions).
- Having to rework merged PRs due to subtle bugs.

**Q: What if I have an "in-flight" bounty?**

If you believe you have an in-flight request or a previously approved bounty that you are currently working on, **Please contact your maintainer.** We will honor those commitments.

### For ecosystem maintainers

As our ecosystem has matured, the importance of ecosystem integrations (Flutter, Qt, Compose, React Native, etc.) has increased in an unprecedented way.
We nevertheless must recognize it.
Ecosystem maintainers keep MapLibre integrations up to date and extend our community in this way.
There are many integrations, and our core team cannot realistically maintain them.
We reserve exclusive merch for these maintainers.
If you face practical limitations, like missing a device for testing, we’ll try to help within reason.
We can also cover reasonable travel costs for presenting your work at conferences (contact a board member first).
At this stage, we cannot provide regular salaries.
We are exploring grant-based funding options - if you’re interested in submitting a grant proposal, contact a [board member](/about), and we’ll support you in writing a successful proposal.

## Looking ahead

MapLibre exists because of its contributors - across code, ecosystem integrations, and community work.
That hasn’t changed.

This shift is about being honest about what we can sustain today, while focusing our resources where they have the most impact.
By recognizing high-quality contributions, supporting ecosystem maintainers, and making targeted hires, we believe we can better serve both the project and its community in the long term.

MapLibre remains a community-driven project.
If you have feedback or ideas, we want to hear from you.
This is an ongoing conversation, and we’re building the future together.
